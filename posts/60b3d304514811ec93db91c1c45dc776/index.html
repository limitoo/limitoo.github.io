The pill came to Australia in 1961. How far have we come since then?

Contraception has been, and still is, a game changer for women around the world.

But the risks and side effects that come with it can take a real toll on a woman's quality of life.

So, in 2021 should we be demanding more than what's on offer — including sharing more of the load with men?

When the conversation about rare blood clots and the AstraZeneca vaccine was happening, the risk of taking the pill was used as a comparison of clot risks in everyday life.

While people used that argument as a way to demonstrate that the risk of the vaccine was less than other drugs, it also prompted the question: why should women have to take that risk daily? And, while we're at it, also put up with something that changes their mood, their weight and themselves?

That feeling is what led Lizzie Strick to look into whether the pill was still right for her.

She went on the pill at 15 to help treat a condition that caused significant pain in the middle of her cycles, but after gaining more weight than she was comfortable with she decided to try a range of different types of the pill.

Lizzie Strick went on the pill when she was a teenager.(Supplied)

Then, at 26, she decided to come off it.

"One thing I found really surprising is something that I thought was quite normal was, I actually thought I was a quite detached person emotionally," she says.

"I would experience emotions [like] joy, but I still felt a little bit detached from joy in some cases.

"And I used to always get brain fog, I just thought that was normal - that's what I'd experienced, probably since I was 15."

Coming off the pill, she said, "all of a sudden, emotions felt so much more real". Lizzie says she became more productive at work and overall her mood improved.

But she was faced with a new issue of what contraception to use next.

After looking into a few different options, none were the right fit — either she was advised not to have certain types because of the risk of heavy bleeding (Lizzie's anaemic) or she wasn't comfortable with how they could affect her mood and weight.

Lizzie says she felt like she had brain fog on the pill.(Supplied)
Lizzie Strick came off the pill originally at 26. (Supplied)

"While it is case by case with what works for people, there's overwhelming consensus [in my friendship group] that there are lots of negative side effects for all contraception," Lizzie says.

"There needs to be more of a conversation on how can you have a safe sexual experience, and what other alternatives are there because contraception is all about choice right?"

Right indeed, says Family Planning NSW, who are banging on the door of government to expand the options in Australia.

Months before the clotting issue came up, Family Planning NSW made a submission to a federal inquiry into what new medicines the government should be investing or funding research in, and basically called for it to step up when it comes to contraception.

It's worth noting here that contraception is used by women of all ages to both prevent pregnancies, but also sometimes to help manage other conditions like endometriosis or treat symptoms of perimenopause (like heavy bleeding or irregular periods).

"We do have a decent range of contraceptive options for people in Australia ... but we don't have as many options as some women do in other countries and we can be slow to get into some of these options," says Dr Deb Bateson, medical director at Family Planning NSW.

"We need to work out how we can make sure that women in Australia can access some of these other excellent methods that are available overseas or in the pipeline."

What are these alternatives or things in the pipelines you ask? We'll get to them in a moment, but first here's a quick wrap of the main options on offer.

What's the current state of play?
Contraceptives illustration 1 of 3(ABC News: Emma Machan)

The pill(s): The oral contraceptive pill was first introduced to Australia in February 1961 and, while the most recent data is now a couple of years old, it's historically been the most-used contraceptive in Australia.

The most common is the combined estrogen and progestogen pill, but progestogen-only pills — often called "mini pills" are also available and don't lead to clotting issues. For the pill to be effective, women have to make sure they take it at the same time every day.

The side effects reported by women on the combined pill can include irregular bleeding, headaches, skin changes and mood changes. It also increases the risk of developing deep vein thrombosis blood clots, heart attack, stroke and, in some instances, breast cancer.

IUD and implants: Inter-uterine devices, or IUDs, are the most common long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) on the market. There are two different types - non-hormonal copper IUDs, which are toxic to sperm and eggs, or hormonal ones that prevent sperm from getting into the uterus and can sometimes prevent ovulation.

A contraceptive implant is one that's inserted under the skin — usually in a woman's arm — and releases a small amount of the hormone progestogen that prevents pregnancy. In Australia there's only one option on the market.

Both the IUD and the implant can only be inserted and removed by a specially trained doctor or nurse. IUDs usually last between five and 10 years, and the implant three years.

Side-effects of both LARCs vary, but women can experience irregular or heavy bleeding, pain during insertion and removal, and in rare cases, IUDs can damage the wall of the uterus or lead to infections.

Vaginal rings and injections: Vaginal rings and contraceptive injections are, according to a 2016 study, the least-used methods in Australia.Rings work in much the same way that the pill does, but instead of taking a tablet every day a woman inserts the squishy ring herself, leaves it in the vagina for three weeks and then removes it for a week. The ring is replaced with a new one every month. Currently, there's only one vaginal ring available in Australia.

The contraceptive injection is when a woman has progestogen injected, usually in her arm, every 12 to 14 weeks to prevent ovulation.

Both the ring and injection carry similar side effects to other hormonal contraception, like irregular bleeding and mood changes. Some women find keeping the ring in place tricky, while there is a chance the injection may cause a drop in bone density if it's used for an extended period of time.

Increasing options asap is ideal, but given what we have at the moment Dr Bateson's want pay and training for nurses and GPs improved, so more can do things like insert IUDs and make them more accessible for women who want them.

"In general practice, where the majority of contraception is provided, there's no funding mechanism for that to happen, and certainly in rural areas, nurses are a fantastic workforce, but we're not utilising them," she says.

But she also thinks that broader education is needed to make sure GPs are starting conversations with women and giving them the most up-to-date advice possible.

Dr Deb Bateson has been working in sexual health for over 20 years.(ABC News)

"I think it's raising awareness. It's also we, amongst doctors, don't want people just to go along and get the same pill that they've always had because they haven't been given that information about other options," Dr Bateson says.

It's something that Lizzie thinks would've helped her as well.

"Every time I'd go to the GP and say 'I just want to renew my script for the pill' ... they'd often just write the script and say, 'see you later bye'," she says. 

"There wasn't actually a conversation about are you having any of these symptoms? Or have you thought about trying different pills, it's only when I sort of brought up as, 'hey, I'm noticing I've got a little bit of weight, I would like to try a different pill' that the conversation started."

Another of Family Planning NSW and Dr Bateson's recommendations was to change the regulatory process to streamline approvals of other forms of contraception available overseas in Australia.

Crucially, it's not about lowering the bar or not making sure these things are safe, but instead sharing information internationally and coordinating policies for clinical trial approvals, meaning you could do one trial and have multiple countries use that information.

What are the alternatives?

In its inquiry, the federal government was specifically looking at new medical technologies that it should or could be bringing here.

Like Lizzie, Octavia Griffiths has tried just about every contraceptive on the market in the UK, primarily to help with the symptoms of her endometriosis.

Octavia Griffiths wearing her contraceptive patch while she meditates. (Supplied: Octavia Griffiths)

"My body was just really unhappy on most things that I tried," the 26-year-old says.

"Contraception, or you know hormone replacement therapy, is something that's really important in my life. I can't not take the medication on a daily basis."

The 26-year-old, who's now a life coach specialising in helping people with chronic illness around the world, ended up on the patch after a conversation with her GP.

"I was experiencing side effects on the pill that were actually due essentially to the way we process that in our bodies," Octavia says.

Octavia Griffiths isn't ashamed of wearing the patch.(Supplied)

So, to get around the issues she was having metabolising the pill, her doctor recommended giving the patch a go, given the hormones, which are the same as those in the combined oral pill, go straight into the bloodstream.

"The symptoms and the side effects went away because of the way it's administered," Octavia says.

"The only downside, because it's good to be fully honest about these things, is that as someone with sensitive skin, I would say about one in every 20 patches that I use on a weekly basis maybe give me a little bit of a rash underneath.

"I'll just take it off and switch arms, you know, and that's fine."

While Octavia isn't shy about her patch, she admits for some people it might not be something they want to flaunt, quickly pointing out you can wear it in discrete places that few will ever see.

Octavia Griffiths shows how she replaces her contraceptive patch.

Despite the occasional rash, for Octavia the benefits of the patch have had an enormous impact on her life.

"When I was struggling to find something that worked for me, my quality of life was so low — I would need a wheelchair on days just to be able to function," she says.

"The patch has been life-changing for me.

"Finding a medication that allows me to live my life as a normal, average girl ... I mean it literally has changed my life."

The patch is currently available in the UK, parts of Europe and the US, among other countries. The company that sells it didn't reply to a request to see if they have any plans to try and introduce it in Australia.

Earlier this year, Shellie Zeigler, who lives in Jackson, Mississippi, in the US, was at her annual OBGYN appointment and asked about what alternatives were on offer instead of the pill she was on.

Shellie Zeigler was encouraged to try annual ring by her doctor.(Supplied)

The reason was kind of two-fold.

Firstly, Shellie had no interest in getting her period once a month, so skipped the sugar pills on her packets, meaning she would go through more than the allotted 12 a year her insurance would cover.

"I was always kind of running short, and there'd be a few days before my insurance would pick up and pay for my birth control pill ... and it was very stressful," she says.

Secondly, she says the pill wasn't always "compatible" with some of the other medications she takes.

"It might increase my blood pressure or cause headaches and things like that," Shellie says. 

Shellie Zeigler says the annual ring took the stress out of getting contraception.(Supplied)
Shellie says she found inserting the ring was easy.(Supplied)

While at her appointment, she asked about a year-long vaginal ring that she saw on a chart hung up on the wall and, after a conversation about it, decided to give it a go.

"[My doctor] said I could insert it and then leave it in for a year, and unless you take it out you don't have a period," Shellie says.

"And I love that for two reasons: one is that I don't have to deal with my insurance except for one time. And [second] I don't have to go back to the pharmacy every three months to go get more.

Shellie's since stopped using the ring but still thinks it's a great option.

Shellie Zeigler showing what the annual vaginal ring looks like.

As for how easy and comfortable having the ring was, Shellie says that, at least for her, was a breeze.

"I'm in control of it, I put it in, I take it out when I want to," she says.

"You know, it's not like an IUD where you have to go to a doctor and have it put in and removed.

"I'm not going to say it's perfect, because I have read how some women have had trouble inserting it, maybe it coming out, but I personally have not had any of those problems."

Shellie liked the way the ring didn't interfere with other medications.(Supplied)

While women in Australia don't have to worry about the insurance aspect of contraception, the annual ring means women would only have to get a script filled once a year, and it's a more sustainable alternative.

The company that makes the annual vaginal ring, Population Council, says it's keen on bringing it to Australia but is in the process of finding a partner to facilitate that.

You might be thinking "wait, I'm on a POP at the moment, that's not new?". 

While the "mini-pill", a kind of progestogen-only pill (POP), is available in Australia, a different kind of progestogen-only pill is available in places like the UK and New Zealand.

Unlike a mini-pill, a desogestrel progestogen-only pill (bear with the hormone names) contains a different dose of hormones that often also stops ovulation.

Crucially, it takes a little bit of the burden off women who usually have to take the mini-pill within three hours every day. 

New Zealand Family Planning national nurse adviser Rose Stewart says it's another option they have to give to women who may not be able to take the combined oral pill, but also struggle with remembering to take a tablet bang on time.

What's in the works?

At this point, you might've thought once or twice: "What about the men in our lives? Where's the options for their different contraception?"

Potentially unsurprisingly, other than the condom and vasectomy there isn't anything currently on the market in Australia for men contraception-wise.

In good news, though, there's quite a few studies at the moment — some of which are getting close to clinical trials in Australia — into reversible contraception for men.

The projects include injections to stop sperm being ejaculated, implants, pills, a gel men can rub on their arms, and even a device that uses ultrasound to temporarily immobilise sperm.

The COSO is a mug-sized device that uses ultrasound to immobilise sperm but is still in development.(Source: supplied)

Interestingly, a lot of this research is focusing on non-hormonal methods that don't bring with them unwanted side effects.

However, even as research and support from men to be an equal part of the contraception picture grows, there are still barriers to overcome. As one 2019 paper put it: "Research in the last decade has been slow and commercialisation is not on the horizon".

Professor Juliet Richters, who's worked in sexual health for 30 years, also thinks sexism still plays a role in why it's taking so long to get male contraceptives off the ground.

So, what about other options for women?

The new vaginal ring, which has a physical barrier in it, could be in Phase 3 trials next year.(Supplied: Dare Bioscience)

Globally, there's ongoing development of different combinations of hormonal contraception that's already on the market.

When it comes to non-hormonal alternatives, research has been patchy and a bit stop-start.

But a growing push from women for more choice seems to be having an impact — with drug companies beginning to look at more innovative ways to prevent pregnancy.

In the US, the Food and Drug Administration has actually approved a non-hormonal gel that's inserted into the vagina, using an applicator similar to a tampon, that keeps the vagina acidic and immobilises any sperm that come into contact with it.

"The global research effort is driven by women's needs, and that is them saying, 'look, we want methods that have lower hormones or different hormones or no hormones at all'," Dr Bateson says.

"We must make sure that women in Australia have that equitable access to these new options [when they become available]."

How do we get more options here?

There's no quick and easy answer to that.

Family Planning NSW wants the government to simplify what it and some companies describe as an "onus" regulatory process to introduce contraceptives here, like coordinating policies on clinical trial approvals with other countries.

The committee overseeing the government's inquiry has recognised this is an issue for more than just contraception, recommending in its final report that there should be changes to the TGA's fee structures and processes to "encourage companies to enter the Australian market with their products and technologies".

Broadly speaking the government's stance is that it’s very keen to see more contraception options on the market, but it's up to drug companies to make the applications, get the approval and then, if it's recommended, have it put on the PBS and covered by Medicare.

Improving access and uptake of Long Acting Reversible Contraception (LARCs) is also one of the government’s key measures of success in the latest National Women's Health Strategy.

When asked if it would follow the recommendation from Family Planning NSW to put money into expanding the number of nurses and GPs who can insert things like IUDs, the government pointed to $1.2 million in funding in 2019 for a primary care support network to improve the delivery of LARCs.

It's also hard to pinpoint exactly how much funding the government's putting toward new technologies because a lot of the programs it backs are aimed at women's sexual and reproductive health more broadly.

Professor Richter says government could play a key role in that area, given drug companies can be hesitant to put money into anything too different in case the side effects end up being worse than what we have at the moment.

"They're tweaking [current methods] but they're not going back to the lab and starting again from scratch," she says.

"When you're giving something with nothing wrong with them, or nothing that you know about, the standards are much higher."

Lizzie Strick says it's everyone's responsibility to keep the conversation on contraception going.(Supplied)

The other way is — regardless of your gender — to keep banging on about wanting more choice. That could be to your GP, on social media, to your friends or to drug companies themselves.

The uptick in research into men's contraception and non-hormonal options is because, in part, of people making it known they want things to change.

Lizzie's advice is that we need to keep talking about contraception, regardless of how slow the movement on alternatives is, to help others while we can now.

"I think it's more than important. I think it's our responsibility," she says.

"I think it's absolutely vital that the whole society is educated on this."
