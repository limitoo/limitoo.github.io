'She said no, do them again': Trinity trained so hard at gymnastics her vertebra broke

A 13-year-old shouldn't be world-weary, disillusioned. And under most circumstances, Trinity de Lance is a vivacious and whip-smart young teenager.

But when talk turns to gymnastics, her tone changes.

The de Lance household seems to be storing a gym'sworth of disused equipment. There's a fold-up beam under the couch, a horizontal bar under a tarp out the front, gym mats lie around because there's no room to store them, and in the cupboardsthere are enough leotards, carefully stored in plastic sheaths, to outfit an entire squad of little girls.

Trinity started gymnastics at four years old, but as she got older and better, the sport became increasingly intense.(ABC News: Marty McCarthy)

It wasn't always this way. The little girl who started gymnastics when she was just four loved the sport.

"I think it was just the thrill to fly," she said.

She was good too.

Before long she was in elite squads competing in state and national championships and training up to 32 hours a week.

But when things started getting serious, attitudes started changing the coaching was obviously stricter, but there were other changes particularly when it came to injuries.

Trinity says she didn't tell her parents a lot of what went on at gym, because she thought she'd get into trouble from her coaches.(Supplied)

And when she did, her parents wouldn't know.

"Because I knew I'd get in trouble for it,from coaches,like I had no business telling my parents."

But there was one time she couldn't keep quiet.

A backwards walkover is one of those manoeuvres that gymnasts make look graceful but is in reality a brutal example of body contortion.

Lean over backwards until your hands touch the ground behind your back, then kick your legs over, pivoting on your hands, to land facing in the same position.

Early last year, Trinity arrived at a morning training session and was told to do five walkovers on a beam.

The coach wasn't happy, so she did another five.

By now her back was hurting.

"To a point where I was like on the floor crying because it hurtso much, and she would still say no, do it again."

Stieve and Trinitysay compaints they've made about the treatment Trinity received while traininghave fallen on deaf ears.(ABC News: Marty McCarthy)

By the time she'd done around 30 or 40, she said she was in excruciating pain.

Her mother, Stievede Lance, takes up the story.

They got X-rays, ultrasounds and an MRI.

"What it revealed was that she had chipped her vertebracase with blunt force injury by arching back so much that her vertebrae are actually touching, and they are just constantly being banged together in a way that took the top of the bone off her vertebra," Stieve said.

The vertebrahad been broken in two places. She was 12, and her gymnastics career had affectively ended.

Trinity doesn't think she's going to recover, physically or emotionally, from her treatment.(Supplied)

Since her injury Trinity has spent months doing physio to try to rehabilitate her injury, but she says she is still not fully recovered, still feels some pain, and has regular back spasms.

"I don't think I'm everreally going to recover," the 13-year-old said.

'Neither substantiated or unsubstantiated'

The family complained to the club as well as the state gymnastic association and Gymnastics Australia (GA), but they said their allegation that Trinity had been trained beyond the point of injury fell on deaf ears.

But last September a new avenue appeared one that coincided with GA commissioning the Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC) to investigate decades' worth of alleged abuses in the sport.

The AHRC report came out in May this year, finding the sport had enabled a culture of physical, emotional and sexual abuse which many participants described as toxic.

But it would be incorrect to think those accusations of abuse belong in the distant past.

For reasons of transparency, GA said that while the commission was investigating, all complaints would be handled by a new and independent body that had just been set up by the federal government, Sport Integrity Australia (SIA).

SIA heard of some very recent allegations of serious physical and psychological abuse of children.

Stieve de Lance was one of 35 complainants who took advantage of a four-month window to make an allegation to SIA.

"We thought maybe this one time, we would be believed because the Human Rights Commission clearly stated that there is a toxic culture in gymnastics, and that athlete abuse was widescale," Stieve said.

Stieve and Trinity were both interviewed at length by SIA.

"I found it quite competitive, combative, it was definitely like a police interview," Stieve said.

"The investigators were good,but they repeatedly told us that they were quite limited in what they were allowed to investigate."

Of the 35 complaints SIA received, seven were assessed as worthy of an investigation.

Stieve and Trinity were both interviewed at length by SportIntegrity Australia.(ABC News:Lori Youmshajekian)

Four of those,includingStieve and Trinity de Lance's complaint, werereturned with a finding of "neither substantiated or unsubstantiated".

That finding came in the form of a letter from GA along with a reminder that complainants had an obligation to maintain confidentiality.

Now, those four families say they feel that the process has let them down.

They're ignoring the warning and speaking up, alleging that their complaints weren't properly heard or judged.

'I feel like they didn't believe me'

Their case has been taken up by the newly formed Athlete Rights Australia an advocacy group formed by three former elite child gymnasts who all say they were abused during their time in the sport.

One of them is Sophie Vivian, who says she was abused as a child while training at the AIS.

"There seems to be instances where key evidence wasn't considered, key witnesses weren't interviewed, the families feel as though the process wasn't thorough enough,"Dr Vivian said.

Sophie Vivian is a former elite gymnast and one of the founders of Athlete Rights Australia.(ABC News: Tom Hancock)

"You can't be a credible witness and provide MRI evidence and doctor's letters and it not be substantiated," Stieve added.

"Either an X-ray is wrong, and a doctor is a liar, and we are liars, or not. I don't understand how you can have a non-result."

Trinity said she felt "defeated during the whole process", that she wasn't believed.

The ABC asked SIA for an interview to explain the findings, but the organisation declined, instead sending a written statement in response to questions which included: how did it come up with afinding of "neither substantiated or unsubstantiated"?

In response, SIA wrote: "Our job is to assess all evidence and determine whether the evidence shows that one version of events was more likely than another to have occurred.

"If there is not enough evidence to support one version of events over another (to the burden of proof required), or if there are other issues (such as a person or incident being out of jurisdiction of the policy), a matter might be categorised as neither substantiated or unsubstantiated."

'We were absolutely silenced and shut down'

15-year-old Scarlett Magnanini has also just been through the process of complaining to SIA, and like Trinity de Lance, she also feels let down.

The ABC originally approached the former gymnast's parents, but it was Scarlett herself who wanted to be interviewed.

Scarlett Magnanini was a gifted young gymnast, who says she was belittled and personally attacked.(ABC News: Marty McCarthy)

"I feel if someone needs to make a complaint, or if they feel that something's wrong, it should be listened to," she said

"I just can't imagine that happening to anyone else it's terrible. And I hope that through doing this, that it can be fixed, and that people will be listened to."

Like Trinity de Lance, Scarlett was a gifted young gymnast, who trained up to 30 hours a week.

Scarlett as a young gymnast.(Supplied)

"Oh, it was tough. It's a very demanding sport, you have to be really fully committed and at that time, I definitely was, it was my passion," she said.

And also, like Trinity, it was an injury that first derailed Scarlett's passion.

When she was 12, Scarlett injured her foot badly in a fall.

She thought it was broken and secretly hoped that was the case so that she wouldn't have to keep training.

However, scans revealed the foot wasn't broken, although she had extensive ligament damage in her toes.

"Oh, God, I think I actually almost cried. I actually wanted nothing more than to hear that [it was broken]," she said.

"Because it was very much seen that if it wasn't a break, it wasn't an injury."

She started to lose trust in her coach and lose her passion for gymnastics.

Feeling broken, Scarlett couldn't concentrate at school and felt emotionally down.(ABC News: Marty McCarthy)

Then, she said, there was the psychological abuse.

"You know, belittling and attacks andyelling,putting down my personal character," Scarlett said.

One time, three months after she injured her foot, stands out.

Her coach called all the girls into a smaller "ballet room" next to the gym, Scarlett explained.

"There's six of us sitting in the room, as well as my coach.

"She shuts the door behind us and straightaway, there's this personal attack that starts she's yelling, hysterical.

Scarlett says she became scared of her coach, but didn't tell her parents immediately.(Supplied)

"I was so scared. I didn't know how far she was going to take it at that point. Some of the girls, you know, they were crying. And I wasterrified of what she was going to do next," Scarlett said.

She didn't tell her parents immediately because "in the past she threatened that if I told anyone, I'd be punished for it".

But eventually the truth came out: Scarlett's parents noticed her behaviour changing, while they got messages from other parents who had heard what had gone on from their own girls.

It was a watershed moment for Scarlett and her parents as they uncovered what Scarlett alleged was a pattern of abuse.

"I thought that, in that point, it was a normal thing, and it wasn't until I started telling my parents then that I realised, no, it wasn't normal at all.

"It hada massive effect. I felt that I was carrying it almost everywhere I went. I was struggling a bit in school, focusing, and I was very quiet. I was really down," she said.

The SIA investigation into Scarlett's complaint came back with a finding of "neither substantiated or unsubstantiated".(ABC News:Lori Youmshajekian)

Her parents complained to the club but say nothing came of it, and so when the chance came to tell their story to Sport Integrity Australia, they put in another complaint alleging Scarlett was the victim of physical and psychological abuse.

They supplied doctor's reports and the names of witnesses to the alleged "ballet room incident" as it came to be known.

The SIA investigation came back with a finding of "neither substantiated or unsubstantiated", which left Scarlett shattered.

"I felt like when that answer came out, my experience wasn't validated, it wasn't accepted.

'Those findings don't logically make sense'

Donna-Louise Wilson's daughter, Jamie, trained at the same gym and under the same coach as Scarlett Magnanini, but is a few years younger.

Donna-Louise Wilson's daughter developed anxiety and erratic behaviour, which she says doctors linked to gymnastics.(ABC News:Marty McCarthy)

When Jamie was nine, Donna-Louise began to notice changes in her child's behaviour. Always quiet, she became virtually non-verbal, and several times she ran away from gym or home.

Over time, Jamiebecame more anxious and her behaviour more erratic andrisky. The whole family was on edge.

She was placed in crisis accommodation, but it wasn't until her psychologist noticed that Jamie became particularly stressed when she talked about gymnasticsthat her mental health team put two and two together.

Over time, Donna-Louise says she heard stories about Jamie's coach from her younger daughter, who was at the same club.

"She would get up very close in the child's face and be absolutely screaming," Donna-Louise said.

She complained to SIA about the club and the coach.

Ms Wilsonalso complained to SIA about the treatment herdaughter Jamie received from her coach andclub.(Supplied)

"She was yelling at the children, she's isolating them, she was humiliating them," she said.

They submitted doctor's reports linking Jamie's anxiety and erratic behaviour with gymnastics, and cited several witnesses.

Again, a letter came back from Gymnastics Australia finding some of the findings were "neither substantiated or unsubstantiated", while other allegations were found to be unsubstantiated.

It left Donna-Louise dumbfounded.

Jamie's mother say she and her daughter were left traumatised by the whole process.(Supplied)

"I'm quite confounded by those findings, because they don't logically make sense."

Moreover, she said the whole process including a lengthy interview with two investigators was very taxing for the whole family, particularly Jamie, who is now 12.

"It was very overwhelming and very intimidating, because this is two years after the trauma events," Donna-Louise said.

"To have to relive all of that was very, very difficult for the girls.

'This will always be a challenging space'

SIA's statement to the ABC said: "This will always be a challenging space, because we have to appreciate that not everyone will be satisfied with the outcome."

"All we can do is apply the process fairly and make determinations based on the evidence."

But critics of the system that has been set up say that misses the point.

One of them is Matt Graham, the director of legal and player relations at the global peak body for athlete unions World Players.

Matt Graham says when young people are involved, the processes need to have a certain empathy about them.(Supplied)

"What we see with these cases,is unfortunately, some survivors who have gone through a really horrible experience, and this finding of 'non-substantiated' really doesn't give them any sort of remedy at all," he said.

Sport Integrity Australia was created out of the old Australian Sports Anti-Doping Authorityand arms of the federal government's Sport Australia.

It still carries out anti-doping responsibilities but can also investigate integrity matters relating to issues such as match-fixing and claims of abuse.

The problem, as Matt Graham sees it, is that it's set up to operate like a police force, complete with interrogative interviews.

And that doesn't suit survivors of trauma,particularly when they're children.

"It's just fundamentally flawed, and really, itsort oflacks empathy," he said.

"The systems have to be based on the rights of survivors, be responsive to their needs, hear their voice, and more broadly, build in and respond to their experiences.

"The problem is what we're seeing is a verylegalistic response to these issues. It's very formal, and it's very cold.

"Young girls, in these casesin their early teens, are in acutely vulnerable situations.

Both Scarlett and Trinity saytheir experience of going through the investigation left them feeling worse.(ABC News:Lori Youmshajekian)

SIA said in its statement that it acknowledgedthat speaking about previous experiences could be extremely difficult.

It said its investigators were trained to meet Australian Government Investigation Standards and that children were always interviewed with an adult.

Both Scarlett Magnanini and Trinity de Lancesay that despite those safeguards, their experience of going through the investigation left them feeling worse than before.

"Doing that interview with Sports Integrity Australia, it was a tough thing to have to then relive, in depth, this experience," Scarlett said.

"But I thought, OK, this is good, because from this, I'm going to get something out of it and she's going to be held accountable for her actions."

"It made me feel so much worse then knowing that, like, what did that really do?" Trinity De Lance added.

Matt Graham says best-practice complaints processes should include some form of remedy.

"It can take the shape of reconciliation, often the survivors will want to have their relationship with the sport restored," he said.

"In other cases, it could be an apology and some sort of acknowledgement of wrongdoing."

Stieve de Lance says she and Trinity were told they had no avenue for appeal, and there has been no offer of support from GA.

That angers Stieve, who says Trinity has been left with emotional as well as physical scars.

"She has panic attacks, she gets anxious, and they can't even bring themselves to ask if she's OK," she said.

Dr Vivian is prepared to cut SIA some slack.

"I think in this case Sport Integrity Australia is potentially building the plane while they're flying it," she said.

"But they're a body that we need.

Dr Vivian and Athlete Rights have taken up the cases.(ABC News: Tom Hancock)

SIA will increasingly play a central role in handling complaints about sport in Australia.

Just recently Football Australia signed on to its national framework a plan for SIA to act as the independent judge rather than allow sports to investigate themselves.

Gymnastics is expected to sign up shortly, as are other sports.

Matt Graham says SIA hasto get its processes right if it's going to be seen as truly independent and in his words "empathetic".

He citeda study by World Players that found 60 per cent of athletes hadexperienced some sort of abuse in sport, and more than a third of those had experienced some sort of physical abuse.

"So, if Australia wants to be true to its claims of putting athletes first and celebrating their achievements, I think it's really time that we realise this opportunity and get a proper body in place which can take into account independence, but also, more importantly, the needs of survivors and have trauma-informed processes in these systems," he said.

When stories of abuse in gymnastics began to appear in the media, it was often athletes who had been out of the sport for years who spoke up.

It was the distance of time that had empowered them, as the fear of speaking up against a system that would punish whistleblowers slowly receded.

The parents of gymnasts can and still do fear retribution if they speak out.

It's telling, then, that this time, young teenage girls have decided that they want to speak up and have their stories heard.

"I find it important that if you can hear something directly from the person that it happens to, I feel like it holds so much more meaning and value,"Scarlett said.

"I really hopeby speaking out that change is going to occur, and this system is going to be fixed."

"I want those people to know what they did to me," Trinity said.

"I just want them to know that, that they have to live with that.
