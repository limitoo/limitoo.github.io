Figuring out the stats on coronavirus in Wales
A patient gives the thumbs up after receiving his vaccine booster in Cwmbran
What can different statistics tell us about coronavirus in Wales?
Here we update some of the key areas of data for measuring the pandemic and we also try to explain some of the different measures being used.
Wales moved to alert level zero on 7 August, hailed as a "significant moment".
Ministers in October said the most likely scenario in the months ahead was that Wales slowly moves out of the pandemic to a position where coronavirus becomes a seasonal illnes.
How many people are in hospital with coronavirus?
This is now the key indicator and, so far, during the third wave hospital numbers remained relatively low, although they have been rising over the past couple of weeks.
There were 516 hospital patients with confirmed Covid on 1 November - including 116 in Cwm Taf Morgannwg hospitals and 113 in the Cardiff and Vale health board area.
The highest rise in admissions was in Cwm Taf Morgannwg, up by 25 cases in the past 10 days.
The trend remains upwards, rising by 12.1% since 21 October.
However, it does appear to have slowed and is half the rate of increase seen at the beginning of October.
Including suspected Covid and recovering patients, the number across Wales stands at 767, according to Digital Health and Care Wales. That's 9.1% of all patients in hospital.

There were 73 patients in critical care beds with Covid. These included 20 patients in Cardiff and Vale, 13 in Cwm Taf Morgannwg and Betsi Cadwaldr, with 11 patients in Hywel Da health board hospitals and seven in Swansea Bay health board. These are the highest levles since February.
Units are operating above normal non-pandemic capacity, with 110 non-Covid critical patients, showing hospitals are still very busy.
The mortality rate of a critical care Covid patient, in the most recent study of 309 Welsh patients since May, is about 23%, including those who died later in a general ward. About half had been discharged from hospital. The study also shows patients admitted in the past year who survive are spending less time in critical care than earlier in the pandemic.
Covid admissions are at an average of 45 a day, slightly up on two weeks ago (40) and a week ago (38) and have been within a similar range for a month. The average is currently 3.6% of all hospital admissions.
One indication of pressure on the health service is overall bed occupancy - and in acute hospitals this average over the past week has been 88.8% - slightly lower than earlier in October.

Vaccinations and hospital admissions
How is Wales doing on booster jabs?
Wales is currently leading the way on delivering booster vaccinations in the UK.
On 2 November, 17.6% of those aged 12 years old and over in Wales had been given an additional third vaccine dose.
That compares to 16.6% for Scotland, 15% for England, and 6.7% for Northern Ireland.
As of Wednesday, there have been 462,132 Covid booster jabs so far in Wales, and a further 22,854 third doses for those with severely compromised immune systems.
Health boards have been prioritising patients for the booster jabs in the same order as vaccines were originally distributed - with care home residents and staff, health care workers to be targeted first - then those aged 80 and over.
Already, 70% of care home residents have had their booster, while 66.1% of those 80 and over have also received their third vaccine dose.

Hospital admissions rate higher for unvaccinated
The hospital admission rate for younger, unvaccinated people with Covid in Wales is more than four times higher than for those who have been fully vaccinated, latest Public Health Wales analysis shows.
More than 1,120 admissions over the month to 10 October were looked at. It found that the hospital admission rate for those aged between the aged of 18 tor 60 was 16 admissions per 100,000 people for those fully vaccinated - but it rises to 68 per 100,000 for those unvaccinated.
The analysis also reported 44 unvaccinated children were admitted to hospital in the past month.
Looking at admissions for all ages, the admissions rate was more than 58% higher for those unvaccinated.
So why are so many vaccinated people still in hospital?
Unvaccinated patients make up just over 28% of all Covid patients, while 52% have had both doses. But given that more than 78% of those aged 18 to 60 have been fully vaccinated, it means statistically they will still appear prominently in hospital figures.
The sheer numbers of people vaccinated - nearly 95% of those over 60 and 84% of those under 60 - mean that statistically, double-vaccinated people will still be a significant proportion of hospital patients.
But, without the vaccine, those hospital numbers might be even higher and PHW says a second dose of vaccine is 95% protection against being admitted..
Dr Chris Williams, consultant epidemiologist for PHW, said: "A large majority of adults in Wales have now received two doses of coronavirus vaccine, so it is to be expected that a significant proportion of people in hospital will be fully vaccinated.
"Also, the majority of people in hospital are older.
"This group is more likely to be vaccinated, but also are more likely to be hospitalised because of underlying health conditions, reduced immune response, and because they are more clinically vulnerable."

How many cases are there in Wales?
1px transparent line
The landscape for the third wave looks different when compared with the first 143 days of the second wave last autumn.
Daily cases in the third wave have been higher for long periods.
But the number of patients in hospital with confirmed Covid was three times higher in the second wave at this point.
The character of this wave so far has been defined by the age profile. Young people have in the past month have become the largest proportion of Covid cases in Wales.
An undoubted factor is school-age children testing positive through routine lateral flow tests.
According to ONS models for Wales as many as 15% of 12-year-olds in Wales have Covid.
Positive tests linked to schools are falling
Thousands of cases of Covid have been recorded among school pupils and staff since the start of term although numbers have fallen over the past month.
The latest data from PHW, covering the three weeks up to 26 October show there were 10,982 positive tests.
In the latest week, there were 2,105 positive tests linked to schools - a 10% drop from the cases recorded in the previous week.
There have been high numbers of positive LFT results in recent weeks, which began after pupils were asked to take tests before the start of the new school term and then on returning to school.
The number of positive LFTs are still highest in the under-20s, with 6.7% of tests taken proving positive in the latest week. This adds an extra layer which, after follow-up PCR tests, would then start showing up in Covid case rates.

Why do we look at cases per 100,000 going back about five days?
Because of a lag in test results coming back from PHW, we wait for those late results to come in before looking at what the picture is.
It's a bit like in football, waiting on a Saturday evening for all the final scores to come in before looking at the league tables.
The case rate is only a snapshot of the virus out there as it relies on people asking for a test. Others may have the virus and not know it, as they are asymptomatic.
But since the end of summer 2020, when tests were fully available to anyone who thought they might have Covid, it has been a good measure.
Before this testing phase, looking at case rates was less reliable for reading trends.
In this third wave, the significant rise in lateral flow positive tests means an extra layer of asymptomatic cases eventually feed into the number of cases we see, once people take follow-up tests.
Welsh government scientists say now "it is not possible to say what is increased ascertainment (through increased testing) and what is genuine increased incidence."

How do deaths from Covid-19 compare with other causes, like flu?
Covid-19 was the third leading cause of death in Wales in September.
Mortality analysis by the Office for National Statistics showed it accounted for 8.5% of all registered deaths. This compares with 6.6% of all deaths in England.
Covid was the seventh leading cause of death in August and had been ranked as the leading cause of death in six months since the pandemic began.
Heart disease and dementia were the two leading causes of death in Wales in September. Flu and pneumonia were ranked eighth, with the mortality rate significantly lower (31.5%) than normal levels.
So far this year, Covid is joint first leading cause of death in Wales with heart disease. Actual numbers of deaths were exactly the same, 2,885 each in the first nine months of 2021.
The ONS, which takes into account population and age structure, found the mortality rate for deaths due to Covid in Wales in September was 89.6 deaths per 100,000 - significantly higher than the previous month and the highest rate since February. This was just behind the highest English region, the north east of England (89.9).
But there were three health board areas in Wales where the mortality rate in September was higher than the Welsh average - 122.5 per 100,000 in Cwm Taf Morgannwg, Swansea Bay (115.1) and Beti Cadwaladr (107.8).
Looking at deaths from all causes compared to a normal year - so-called excess deaths - these showed they were 19.8% higher than the five-year average.
Across the course of the pandemic, the age-standardised mortality rate for deaths involving Covid is highest in England - 159.1 deaths per 100,000, with Wales on 154, and Scotland (131.3) and Northern Ireland (132.8) both lower in the 19 months to the end of September.
There were 8,312 deaths involving Covid up to the end of September in Wales.
It found with deaths due to Covid over the 19 months, that the mortality rate was 142 deaths per 100,000 in England, compared to 134.8 in Wales.
Broken down, four health board areas of Wales had higher mortality rates due to Covid than the Welsh average, led by Cwm Taf Morgannwg on 217.6 deaths due to Covid per 100,000. This was followed by Cardiff and Vale, Swansea Bay and Aneurin Bevan health boards.
There have been 7,267 deaths due to Covid in Wales over the 19 months.
It was responsible for 6.3% of all registered deaths in March, 22.2% in February and 35.2% in January.

Are deaths really 'due to' Covid-19?
ONS also looks at how deaths are registered on certificates and found in the first 19 months since the pandemic began, Covid was the underlying cause of death - not just a contributory factor - in 87.4% of Covid deaths mentioned by doctors on death certificate in Wales.
So 253 deaths in September were due to Covid out of 289 which were involving Covid.
A doctor's duties include certifying deaths, and this can include any cause in a chain of events leading up to them, including pre-existing conditions and whatever medically makes a contribution.

Which areas have been hit hardest?
Rhondda Cynon Taf has the highest mortality rate over the course of the pandemic in Wales.
It is also the seventh highest of all local authorities across England and Wales.
Six of its communities are also in the highest 20 within Wales, when the death rates are broken down.
A total of 57 deaths in Tonyrefail due to Covid makes it the worst, followed by Sandfields in Port Talbot (52) and Porth East in Rhondda Cynon Taf (48 deaths).
When translated into mortality rates, based on population size, Llanelli Bigyn is the highest, followed by Port Talbot East.
And there is one community remaining which has had no Covid deaths at all through the pandemic - Llandudno Junction South & Llansanffraid Glan Conwy.
In the second wave of the pandemic, Bridgend was the worst-hit area in Wales.

How does Wales compare with the rest of the UK?
On age-standardised mortality rates (ASMR), over the 18 months of the pandemic to the end of August, the mortality rate was higher in England than in Wales, both for deaths involving Covid - and deaths due to Covid.
But if you take a crude mortality rate instead - not accounting for population and age profiles - and based purely on death numbers and population figures, then Wales is higher than in England.
The ONS uses the ASMR in its analysis.
Deaths involving Covid were lower in Scotland and Northern Ireland on both measures.
