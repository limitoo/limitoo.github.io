Sydney's population is predicted to explode — this is where people will live in 20 years

The pandemic lockdowns brought home to Jimmy Mtashar how deprived of services his neighbourhood in outer Sydney is.

"When you realise you couldn't get to a shop within 5 kilometres, or you couldn't get to a recreational park or anything in 5ks, you realise there really is a lack of services in the area," he said.

In Mr Mtashar's suburb of Austral, parks are not the only thing missing. 

He says roads, schools, storm water infrastructure and public transport are all in short supply. 

Austral, in Sydney's south-west, is among Australia's major growth areas.

More than 10,000 people live in Austral and neighbouring Greendale, according to the latest Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) estimate in 2020.

In 20 years, it is expected be home to more than 64,000 people — a 500 per cent increase — taking the prize for the suburb with the biggest projected population growth in NSW.

The number of people who call NSW home is expected to grow by a third in the next 20 years, topping 10.6 million in 2041.

Of those, 3.5 million are predicted to be in regional areas — an increase of 400,000 on today's number.

While suburbs like Austral will bear the brunt of the growth, almost every corner of Sydney is expected to be more crowded.

To house the population, 30,000 to 40,000 new homes need to be built every year for the next five years, with more than 1 million needed in Greater Sydney by 2041, according to NSW government modelling.

To hit those targets, every local government area (LGA) in Sydney has to pull its weight.

The targets range from 16,500 to 18,500 new homes in the Blacktown LGA, to just 150 to 200 in the tiny Hunters Hill LGA on Sydney Harbour.

Some established suburban areas will start to look more like the inner city.

Nearly 30,000 more people will live in Liverpool — which is about 40km from the CBD — by 2041, bringing the population density up to the same level as inner-city Glebe. 

Kathy Cowley says Ku-ring-gai is changing for the worse.(ABC News: Jack Fisher)

On Sydney's north shore, Ku-ring-gai Council has committed to building 3,000 to 3,500 new homes by 2026.

Its leafy suburbs reach up to the national park of the same name.

Lindfield resident Kathy Cowley, president of Friends of Ku-ring-gai Environment, says the 2026 targets are achievable based on developments already approved and existing zoning.

But she says a forecast population growth of 15 per cent up to 2041 is not.

Unlike Austral, where farmland is being rezoned as residential, Ku-ring-gai has limited open space where new homes can be built.

Kathy Cowley says growth in the Ku-ring-gai area is not "ecologically sustainable".(ABC News: Jack Fisher)

"Ku-ring-gai is not a greenfield site. It is similar to many established suburbs burdened with these massive increases that will change the character of our area forever," she says.

The Ku-ring-gai population has grown by around 20 per cent in the past 20 years, which she says has put pressure on infrastructure like schools and roads, and the local community more broadly.

"Over the past 15 years we have seen the wholesale destruction of an extraordinary number of heritage homes, loss of tree canopy, and new homes being pushed into what were bushfire exclusion zones," she says.

Ms Cowley says the growth will also put pressure on the endangered blue gum high forest and Sydney turpentine ironbark forest in the area where most of the development is happening.

"This high growth agenda is clearly not ecologically sustainable in an area of high environmental and heritage significance. In fact, this growth in population is unsustainable," she says.

There are fears the area's environment could be put at risk as its population increases.(ABC News: Jack Fisher)

The NSW population forecasts were made in 2019, before the pandemic hit. 

Since then, there has been a massive rise in the number of people working from home, an exodus from cities to regional areas and closed international borders have caused a drop in people migrating to Australia.

The number of people living in Sydney in 2030 is now forecast to be 337,400 lower than what had been predicted in 2019 as a result of the pandemic, according to the Commonwealth Centre for Population.

Ms Cowley says LGA housing targets should be revised down as a consequence.

A spokesperson for the Greater Sydney Commission — the NSW government agency that sets the housing targets — said projections beyond 2026 were being reviewed in light of updated evidence.

Somwrita Sarkar, an urban data scientist from the University of Sydney, says it's hard to predict how the pandemic will affect population growth, but the geographic shift has already started.

"A big company might decide to have regional outpost offices, which serve as secondary centres, and might have people working from home for two or three days a week," she says.

She says economic investment in regional areas provides an opportunity to take housing pressure off Sydney.

"Sydney is extremely expensive and unaffordable, but if you provide people with good jobs, good places to live, a good lifestyle, then people will go to the smaller towns," she says.

House prices in the Harbour City grew by more than $1,100 a day last year, pushing the median above $1.6 million, according to CoreLogic.

The pandemic saw the largest migration from Australian capital cities to regional areas in 20 years, with the numbers doubling from 2019 to 2020, ABS data showed.

This street in Austral has seen better days.(Supplied: Jimmy Mtashar)

Population growing pains are already being felt in Austral.

Mr Mtashar's two children attend the local public school.

It is now at capacity, with enrolments growing by 25 per cent in the past 12 months.

"Our school deals with the challenge of having continuous new students. We've got a new student this week and new students next week because [people] finish building their home, they move in and they change schools," Mr Mtashar said. 

There is no public high school in Austral and land set aside for one was sold to a private college in 2020.

A spokesperson for the Education Department said it was committed to planning for the future growth in the Austral area to ensure appropriate learning infrastructure was provided.

"In the broader south-west Sydney area, 12 new and upgraded schools have been delivered since the beginning of 2019 with more than six projects still underway," they said.

Mr Mtashar says negotiating traffic jams, pot holes on roads, and flash flooding after storms are all part of daily life.

A spokesperson for Liverpool City Council, which includes Austral in its LGA, said it carried out regular road maintenance, as well as delivering stormwater infrastructure upgrades.

"While Council is working with developers to establish temporary measures for roads and stormwater systems which haven't yet been upgraded, the continuing progress of development in the area will result in a high level of infrastructure being delivered with respect to roads and drainage," they said.

Dr Sarkar says pinpointing where the houses are actually being built, rather than building approvals, would lead to better planning decisions.

"Because we need to know, where do we put the schools and the bus stops and all of that social infrastructure," she says.

A spokesperson for the NSW Planning Department said more than 350 infrastructure projects were underway to support housing growth and the contributions system was being reformed to ensure infrastructure was delivered at the right time.

Mr Mtashar says the next few years in Austral will be difficult.

"We're looking at a number of years of pain and slow growth," he says.

However, he does not regret his decision to build his home there. 

"I wanted to live somewhere where my home is where my heart is," he says.
